# üß≠Defining the Problem Statement

   A problem statement is a high-level summary of a problem/challenge
   It also helps an analyst maintain control when conducting analysis
   What a good problem statement consists of:

      i)   Problem/challenge
      
      ii)  Consequences of not solving it
      
      iii) Benefits of solving it
      
      iv)  Objectives and the end state after it's all solved

# ‚ôªÔ∏èTheory of Change and Logic Models
Theory of Change and Logic Models are both planning and evaluation tools used in healthcare, public services, and non-profits (including ICBs), but they serve slightly different purposes and offer different levels of detail and abstraction.

Here‚Äôs a clear breakdown:

# üß† Theory of Change (ToC)

 # What it is:
A high-level, strategic narrative (often with a visual) that explains how and why change is expected to happen in a particular context. It focuses on causal pathways, assumptions, and contextual factors. More flexible and conceptual than a logic model.

# Key features:
    1Ô∏è‚É£ Emphasises the why behind the change
    
    2Ô∏è‚É£ Begins with the long-term impact and works backward to identify necessary preconditions
    
    3Ô∏è‚É£ Includes external influences and assumptions (e.g. political, social, behavioural)
    
    4Ô∏è‚É£ Often used in complex or system-level interventions
 
# When to use it:
     1Ô∏è‚É£ For strategic planning or programme design
      
     2Ô∏è‚É£ To map out how multiple interventions connect to broad systemic change
      
     3Ô∏è‚É£ To align stakeholders on shared outcomes

# üìä Logic Model

# What it is:

A practical, structured tool that outlines the inputs, activities, outputs, outcomes, and impact of a programme or project. It shows the what and how in a linear or step-by-step way.

# Key features: Focuses on program mechanics.
 
     1Ô∏è‚É£ Begins with inputs/resources and moves forward logically
     
     2Ô∏è‚É£ Less emphasis on assumptions and external factors
     
     3Ô∏è‚É£ Best used for programme implementation, monitoring, and evaluation
     
     4Ô∏è‚É£ Easy to map in Excel or tables
  

# When to use it:

     1Ô∏è‚É£ For operational planning
     
     2Ô∏è‚É£ When designing, tracking, or reporting a project
     
     3Ô∏è‚É£ To define KPIs and success metrics for a single initiative
    

# üéØ Theory of Change vs Logic Model ‚Äî Core Differences

     ‚û°Ô∏è Level of abstraction: ToC is higher-level and strategic; logic models are more detailed and operational
     
     ‚û°Ô∏è Focus: ToC explains why change will happen; logic models describe how it will happen
     
     ‚û°Ô∏è Assumptions and context: Explicit in ToC, often implicit or excluded in logic models
     
     ‚û°Ô∏è Sequence: ToC is often built from outcomes backward; logic models are built from inputs forward
